The algorithms and why we use them

From post production effects to anti-aliasing techniques to computer vision, random sampling algorithms are being used everyday under the hood of many technologies we take for granted. After doing research on random sampling algorithms, I found the three best examples to illustrate are Uniform Random, Mitchell's Best Candidate, and Bridson's Implementation of Poisson Disc. They all use randomness to pick sample points, with varying levels of restrictions. Uniform Random has no restrictions, Best Candidate has some, and Poisson Disc has an absolute restriction on minimum distance between points. We will be using these algorithms to find random sample points on Van Gogh masterpieces to build Voronoi diagrams from the colors of those points. This will illustrate how uniform (or not) these random sample algorithms are. I implemented the algorithms to run and display their points in a 'real time' visualization of themselves at work. We can watch them build their interpretation of Van Gogh's work as a Voronoi Diagram

Self-Portrait, 1887 side by side with Poisson Disc sampled Voronoi Diagram

Voronoi diagram

A voronoi diagram takes a series of points and creates polygons mapped by regions closest to each point. Each pixel within a polygon is closer to it's polygon's sample point than any other. We use the sample point's position within the painting to determine its color, and pass down that color to every pixel within the Voronoi polygon. The result is a beautiful algorithmic interpretation of the colors and positions of the colors of the painting while only needing a small portion of the data of the original image
